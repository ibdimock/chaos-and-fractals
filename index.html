Apollonian Gasket

Introduction

The Apollonian Gasket is a non-self-similar fractal named after Apollonius of Perga, a greek mathematician. The construction of the fractal is very simple, and follows a simple iterative rule. First we start with an exterial bounding circle: $C4$. Inside $C4$ we have three mutually tangent circles $C1$, $C2$, $C3$. The iterative process is as follows:

  1. Pick any three mutually tangent circles: $A$, $B$, $C$.
  2. There exists a circle, $D$, internally tangent to $A$, $B$, and $C$.
  3. Draw circle $D$.
  4. Repeat for every triple of mutually tangent circles.
The first iteration uses the 4 triples: $(C1, C2, C3), (C1, C2, C4), (C1, C3, C4), (C2, C3, C4)$. The convergence of these iterations is the Apollonian Gasket for that specific starting configuration $(C1, C2, C3, C4)$. Descarte's Theorem

In 1643 René Descarte discovered a relationship between the ratios of the radii of four mutually tangent circles. To properly state the theorem we must first define curvature for circles. Curvature $k$ for a circle with radius $r$ is define simply as:

$k = \pm\frac{1}{r}$.
In our case a negative curvature indicate all the other circles are interior, so only the bounding circle has negative curvature. All the other circles will have positive curvature.
